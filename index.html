Executive Summary

MAAP is a twelve-year-old Australian brand that has punched significantly above its weight in the premium cycling apparel category. With approximately $15M in annual revenue and 161 employees, it operates at a fraction of the scale of Rapha (roughly $120M revenue, 457 employees) yet commands a comparable level of brand heat among serious cyclists. The 2025 partnership with GreenEDGE Cycling, bringing MAAP onto the WorldTour as the technical supplier for Team Jayco AlUla, represents the single most significant strategic move in the brand's history and lands at precisely the right moment: Rapha has exited the WorldTour after ending its EF Pro Cycling partnership, leaving a visible gap in the premium cycling apparel conversation at the sport's highest level.

The brand's physical retail network is impressively global for a business of this size, with nine LaB locations spanning Melbourne, Sydney, Berlin, Amsterdam, Los Angeles, Singapore, Hong Kong, Taipei, and Seoul. In the UK and European market specifically, however, MAAP's physical presence is limited to just two stores (Berlin and Amsterdam), with no UK flagship. This is a notable gap given the brand names the UK as a "major market" and that Rapha, despite closing five clubhouses, still operates the largest premium cycling retail network in the region.

MAAP's product strategy is sound. The range covers road performance (Pro and Evade lines), the growing Alt_Road gravel segment, and off-bike lifestyle, with price points sitting squarely in the premium bracket: jerseys from £155 to £185, bib shorts from £225 to £260. These prices are competitive with Pas Normal Studios and slightly below Rapha's Pro Team tier, while significantly above entry-premium brands like Le Col's Sport range. The brand's investment in proprietary fabric technologies, notably AARTERO Flyte for aerodynamic performance, gives it a credible technical story to tell alongside the aesthetic one.

The competitive landscape is shifting in MAAP's favour. Rapha, the category's dominant player, is in its eighth consecutive year of losses, closing stores and restructuring. Le Col has lost its founder and been acquired by HEAD Group, bringing strategic uncertainty. Gore Wear has exited the market entirely. Meanwhile, Pas Normal Studios is the most direct competitive threat: a near-identical founding vintage (also 2014), a similar aesthetic sensibility, and an aggressive retail expansion programme with seven flagship stores. Assos remains the heritage authority, having secured the EF Pro Cycling WorldTour deal vacated by Rapha. Castelli holds the traditional Italian performance position but lacks the lifestyle brand energy of the newer entrants.

Digitally, MAAP's brand-building instincts have been spot on: 348K Instagram followers (more than the 50-year-old Assos) demonstrates the power of the brand's visual identity and content. The next frontier is organic search, where MAAP's presence doesn't yet match its brand strength. DataForSEO keyword analysis shows 7 top-10 UK rankings for MAAP, compared to 38 for Le Col and 30 for Castelli, representing substantial untapped growth. Women's cycling search terms in particular (48,000+ combined monthly searches for variants of "women's cycling shorts") represent an opportunity to align MAAP's search visibility with its investment in women's cycling through the Liv AlUla Jayco sponsorship. Organic search is the single largest digital growth opportunity identified in this audit.

The biggest strategic question for MAAP in Europe is whether the WorldTour visibility, combined with its existing brand momentum, can convert into meaningful market share growth before the competitive window closes. The brand has the product, the aesthetic, and now the racing credibility. What it needs is a sharper UK/European distribution strategy, a serious investment in organic search visibility, and a clear decision on whether to open a London flagship to challenge Rapha on its home ground.

Brand Overview

Founding and Heritage

MAAP was founded in 2014 in Melbourne, Australia by Oliver Cousins and Jarrad Smith. Cousins brought a design background having worked as a design director at Stussy, Mambo, and Globe. Smith had spent a decade immersed in competitive cycling, racing himself, managing race teams, and producing team gear in Italy. The combination of fashion industry experience and deep cycling credibility gave MAAP a distinctive starting point: an Australian brand that could match the technical standards of European incumbents while bringing a fresh design perspective.

The founding thesis was clear: the premium cycling apparel market was dominated by European brands, and there was space for a new voice. Twelve years on, MAAP has become Australia's largest cycling apparel company, with major markets in the US, UK, Germany, and Southeast Asia.

Operations and Supply Chain

MAAP is headquartered in Port Melbourne, Victoria. The brand designs in Melbourne and manufactures primarily in Italy, sourcing performance textiles from Italian and Swiss fabric mills. This European manufacturing base gives MAAP supply chain proximity to its European markets and access to the same fabric innovation ecosystem as competitors like Castelli and Assos.

Physical Retail: The LaB Network

MAAP operates nine LaB stores globally. These are positioned as community spaces at the intersection of cycling culture and contemporary creative culture, not just retail outlets. Each LaB typically hosts group rides and community events.

Market and Category Context

Market Sizing

Market size estimates for cycling apparel vary considerably by source and definition. The most credible estimates for the global cycling apparel market cluster around $2.3-4.0 billion in 2025, with a CAGR of 6-8% projected through the early 2030s. Europe accounts for approximately 32% of the global market, placing the European cycling apparel market at roughly $750-770 million.

The premium segment (brands pricing jerseys above £100 and bib shorts above £150) represents the fastest-growing slice of the market, driven by cycling's evolution from sport to lifestyle identity. Professional cycling apparel accounts for approximately 60% of the total market, with amateur/recreational making up the remaining 40%.

Key Market Trends

Gravel and Alt_Road Expansion

Gravel cycling continues to grow as a discipline, with dedicated events, media coverage, and consumer demand for versatile apparel that works on mixed terrain. MAAP's Alt_Road line is well-positioned here, as is Pas Normal Studios with its PAS Racing gravel team sponsorship.

E-Bike Influence

Electric bicycles accounted for 67% of the European bicycle market in 2025. E-bike riders are creating demand for apparel that blends performance features with commuter-friendly versatility, a segment where traditional race-focused brands have less relevance but where lifestyle-adjacent brands like MAAP could expand.

Sustainability Expectations

Consumers increasingly expect eco-friendly materials (recycled polyester, organic fabrics) and transparent supply chains. MAAP states that "sustainability defines everything we do," but specific certifications, recycled content percentages, and supply chain transparency reporting are not prominently featured on the website.

Direct-to-Consumer Dominance

Premium cycling apparel has shifted heavily toward DTC models, with brands using physical retail as brand experience rather than primary sales channel. This suits MAAP's LaB concept but puts pressure on wholesale relationships and third-party retail partnerships.

Perceptual Positioning

Brand Positioning: Performance Focus vs Design/Lifestyle Orientation

MAAP occupies a distinctive position: high on both performance credibility (now reinforced by WorldTour presence) and design/lifestyle orientation. Pas Normal Studios sits closest, though with a slightly more minimalist, Scandinavian aesthetic versus MAAP's bolder Australian approach. Rapha has historically owned the "lifestyle" end but has drifted away from pure performance credibility since exiting the WorldTour. Assos and Castelli anchor the performance end with less lifestyle energy.

Points of Parity

Table stakes in the premium cycling apparel category include:

  • Italian or Swiss fabric sourcing and European manufacturing
  • Comprehensive range: jerseys, bibs, jackets, gilets, base layers, accessories
  • Both men's and women's lines with genuine (not afterthought) women's product
  • Road and gravel/adventure sub-ranges
  • Strong Instagram presence with high-quality visual content
  • Community engagement through rides, events, and club programmes
  • Sustainability claims (though depth of commitment varies widely)

MAAP meets all of these parity requirements. No obvious gaps.

Points of Difference

  • Design DNA from fashion, not cycling: Cousins' background at Stussy and Mambo gives MAAP a visual language that competitors can't replicate. The brand looks different because its founders think different.
  • Australian identity in a European-dominated category: A genuine point of differentiation. No other premium cycling brand brings an Australian perspective to product design and brand culture.
  • WorldTour entry at the right moment: MAAP enters the WorldTour just as Rapha exits and Le Col loses its team deal. The timing is fortuitous.
  • Fashion collaborations: The P.A.M. (Perks and Mini) partnership, now four collections deep, brings MAAP into contact with a streetwear/fashion audience that no other cycling brand is reaching.
  • Proprietary fabric technology: AARTERO Flyte aerodynamic fabric is a credible technical story. Publishing wind-tunnel data would strengthen the performance claim further and give media a concrete story to tell.

Value Proposition Canvas

Customer Jobs to Be Done

  • Functional: Perform better on the bike (comfort, aerodynamics, temperature regulation), look fast, have kit that lasts
  • Social: Signal cycling identity and taste to peers, belong to a community of like-minded riders
  • Emotional: Feel confident and motivated when kitting up, express personal style through what you ride in

Customer Pains

  • Premium cycling kit that looks generic or dated
  • Having to choose between performance and aesthetics
  • Brands that feel corporate or soulless despite high prices
  • Inconsistent sizing and fit across brands
  • Sustainability guilt about textile consumption

How MAAP Addresses These

MAAP's value proposition directly addresses the pain of choosing between performance and aesthetics. The brand's design heritage means the visual identity is as considered as the technical specification. The WorldTour partnership validates performance credibility without MAAP needing to sacrifice its design-led positioning. The P.A.M. collaborations address the "soulless" pain point by connecting cycling to broader creative culture.

The area with most room to grow: sustainability communication. MAAP's commitment to sustainability is stated, and the brand clearly cares about this space. The next step is making that commitment visible and measurable, with specific data on recycled materials, supply chain practices, and environmental targets. As Rapha publishes detailed sustainability reports and PNS references sustainable manufacturing, the bar for credible sustainability claims is rising across the category.

Messaging and Communications Review

Homepage Clarity

MAAP's homepage leads with "Curate Your Season" as the hero message, supported by a bundle promotion offering 15% off. Below the fold, the WorldTour Capsule collection and GreenEDGE partnership feature prominently. The navigation splits cleanly into Man/Woman, with secondary navigation for Road, Alt_Road, and Off Bike.

The messaging communicates premium cycling clearly, but it doesn't yet capture what makes MAAP distinctive. "Curate Your Season" is a solid seasonal prompt, though the brand's strongest differentiators (Australian design perspective, WorldTour credibility, fashion-industry heritage) sit deeper in the site rather than leading the homepage experience. A first-time visitor would quickly understand "premium cycling clothing," but the opportunity is to also convey why MAAP specifically, rather than any other premium brand.

Value Proposition Strength

MAAP's stated mission, "committed to developing the art and progression of cycling through a considered approach to style, innovation and performance," captures the brand's ambition well. The opportunity is to sharpen it further. MAAP's real story, the fusion of fashion-industry design thinking with cycling performance, is genuinely distinctive and not something competitors can credibly claim. That story deserves a verbal expression as strong as the visual one.

Brands like Assos ("Total Cycling Comfort") and Rapha ("cycling is a way of life") have distilled their positioning into phrases that are immediately ownable. MAAP has an equally compelling story to tell; the verbal identity just hasn't caught up with the visual one yet.

Consistency Across Channels

The visual identity is exceptionally consistent. MAAP's aesthetic, clean, dark, typographically considered, with bold colour blocking in the product, carries through from website to Instagram to LaB store design. This visual consistency is a genuine strength and one of the reasons the brand's Instagram following is disproportionately large.

The verbal messaging is less consistent. The website uses "developing the art and progression of cycling." The Instagram bio reads "committed to developing the art and progression of cycling." The brand story uses language about "riding makes everything better." These are adjacent but not unified. A sharper verbal identity would reinforce the visual one.

Brand Story

MAAP has a genuinely compelling founding story: two people from Stussy and the Italian racing scene coming together in Melbourne to create something that didn't exist in cycling. That narrative has the same emotional power as Rapha's founding mythology (Simon Mottram, the original manifesto, the first product run), which is now deeply embedded in cycling culture. The About page tells the story, but there's an opportunity to lean into it more boldly across the brand's communications, making it a central pillar of the positioning rather than background context.

!
Messaging opportunity: MAAP's visual brand is premium, distinctive, and consistent. The verbal brand has room to match that standard. MAAP has a stronger story than most competitors; the opportunity is to bring that story forward with sharper positioning language, a more prominent founding narrative, and a verbal platform that captures what makes MAAP different in words as powerfully as the design already does visually.

Technical SEO Findings (click to expand)
  • Hosting: The site runs on Vercel (not Shopify), with HTTP/2 support, valid SSL (Let's Encrypt, expires May 2026), sitemap present, and robots.txt configured. No start page deny flags. These are solid technical foundations.
  • 404 errors: Multiple collection pages (e.g., /collections/man-jerseys, /collections/man-bibs-all, /collections/jerseys, /collections/man) returned 404 errors during research. If these URLs are linked to externally or indexed by Google, they will harm crawl efficiency and user experience. These need immediate 301 redirects to the correct regional collection pages.
  • URL structure: The site uses a regional subdirectory model (/uk/, /eu/, /us/) but the default domain (maap.cc) also serves content. Working collection URLs appear to be under /uk/collections/ for UK visitors. Ensure hreflang tags are properly implemented so Google serves the correct regional version.
  • Keyword cannibalisation risk: The /uk/ and /eu/ versions of product pages may be competing with each other in search results if hreflang implementation is incomplete.
  • Crawl performance: A DataForSEO on-page crawl timed out after 300 seconds with 0 of 50 pages crawled, despite pages being queued. This may indicate server-side rate limiting or slow response times to crawler user agents. Worth investigating whether Vercel configuration or robots.txt crawl-delay directives are throttling crawlers, which would also affect Googlebot.
  • Content gap: The site lacks search-optimised content pages (buying guides, comparison content, FAQ pages) that would target informational queries. All content is either product pages or brand-building editorial stories.

Strategic Implications

This audit reveals a brand in an unusually favourable competitive position. MAAP's challenges are primarily about execution and speed, not fundamental strategy or product quality. The brand has the right product, the right visual identity, and now the right racing platform. The question is whether it can capitalise on a competitive window that won't stay open indefinitely.

Priority 1: Own the WorldTour Moment

The Jayco AlUla partnership is the single most important asset MAAP has for European growth. This needs to be more than a logo on a jersey. Content from race days, product development stories grounded in WorldTour feedback, rider profiles, and behind-the-scenes access should become the backbone of MAAP's European content strategy. The WorldTour Capsule collection is a good start. It needs to be supported by a content engine that turns every Grand Tour, every Classic, every stage win into a brand moment. Rapha built its brand on the mythology of professional cycling. MAAP now has direct access to that mythology.

Priority 2: Solve the UK Distribution Gap

The absence of a UK physical presence is the most obvious strategic gap. A London LaB would serve multiple purposes: brand experience for the UK's largest cycling market, credibility signal for wholesale partners, content creation hub for European storytelling, and a direct competitive statement against Rapha on their home ground. If a full LaB is too capital-intensive in the short term, a pop-up strategy tied to major UK cycling events (RideLondon, The Cycling Show, Goodwood) could build presence while testing demand.

Priority 3: Let the Brand Story Lead

MAAP's visual brand is premium and distinctive. The verbal brand has the ingredients to match it. The founding story (fashion meets cycling, Melbourne meets Italy, design thinking meets race performance) is one of the strongest in the category, and "developing the art and progression of cycling" captures the ambition. The opportunity is to sharpen that into something even more ownable and emotionally resonant, a verbal expression as memorable as the visual identity. Whether that takes the form of a brand platform refresh, a sharper tagline, or simply more confident storytelling across touchpoints, it would amplify everything else MAAP is doing.

Priority 4: Close the Organic Search Gap

This is the most data-backed priority in the audit. MAAP has 7 top-10 UK keyword rankings. Le Col has 38. That gap translates directly into lost traffic and lost sales. The fix is threefold: first, resolve the technical issues (404 errors on collection pages, potential hreflang gaps, and the crawl-blocking behaviour identified by DataForSEO). Second, optimise existing product and category pages for the high-volume terms MAAP is missing, starting with women's cycling shorts (48,000+ monthly searches where MAAP is invisible). Third, build a layer of search-targeted content (buying guides, "best cycling jersey" guides, "MAAP vs Rapha" comparison pages) to capture informational queries that feed the top of the purchase funnel. This content can coexist with the brand-building editorial on maap.cc/stories without diluting it. Given the growing role of AI-generated answers in the purchase journey, structured content with clear specifications and FAQ schema would also improve GEO visibility.

Priority 5: Build Community Infrastructure in Europe

Rapha's RCC, with an estimated 26K members globally, remains the most established cycling community programme globally. PNS runs effective Strava challenges. MAAP's In The Field rides and LaB communities are building something real, but the European programme needs more structure: a membership or loyalty programme, regular ride calendar in key European cities, and Strava challenges tied to product launches and racing moments. Community is the moat that keeps customers coming back after the initial purchase.

Priority 6: Make the Sustainability Story Visible

MAAP clearly cares about sustainability, and "sustainability defines everything we do" signals genuine intent. The next step is making the evidence as visible as the commitment: percentage of recycled materials across the range, carbon footprint per garment, supplier audit results, end-of-life programme. The bar is rising in European markets (CSRD requirements, Green Claims Directive) and consumers are increasingly drawn to brands that show their working. MAAP's Italian manufacturing and premium fabric sourcing are inherently strong sustainability stories; they just need to be told with data.
