Pyramid Day

A day on-site is the best discovery you will ever get. Learn, build trust, spot the one thing worth fixing first. Not to pitch. Listen twice as much as you talk.

01

Before you go

  • Kit: recorder (charged), phone for photos, a real notebook and pen for sketching the org and the workflow, a few business cards.
  • Wear something you can be on your feet in all day. A shoot floor is not a boardroom.
  • Arrive early. First impressions land with the team, not just the founder.
  • No new reading on the way in. The homework is done. Go in curious, not scripted.
02

Your mindset

  • You are a guest learning the business, not a consultant auditing it.
  • Curiosity over cleverness. "Walk me through that" beats any opinion.
  • Everything you learn today makes the discovery sprint faster and the pitch sharper. It counts even before they sign.
03

Traps to avoid

  • Do not diagnose out loud. You will spot problems fast, sit on them.
  • Do not pitch or promise. Today earns the right to pitch later.
  • Do not criticise what they have built, even gently. Ask why it works the way it does.
  • Do not name a price or a timeline. "I will put that in the plan" is the answer.
  • Do not be glued to your phone. Notes are fine, scrolling reads as bored.
04

The photography session

This is your ecommerce operation in the flesh, and the exact grind we think Manual Focus can take off their plate. Quietly note:

  • How many products or colour variants get shot in a day, and the setup.
  • Who shoots, who edits, and what software they use.
  • What happens to the images after: background removal, resizing per channel, colour-matched variants.
  • How long from shoot to a live listing on the site, on eBay, on the retailers.
  • Whether any of it is captured for social or video. If not, that is free content walking out the door.
+
Offer to help. Carrying stock or holding a light buys more candour than any question. Muck in.

Ask on the day

"What happens to these once they are shot?"
"How long until this is live and selling?"
"How do you do all this when a new model lands?"
"What is the most painful part of getting a product online?"
05

Questions to weave in

Keep them conversational. A good chat, not the intake form.

Where they are headed

  • What would make this a great year for the business?
  • The single biggest thing slowing growth right now?
  • Anything off the table, or a line you would not cross?

The operation, the grind

  • Walk me through a new-model launch, first part to live everywhere.
  • How many people touch a product between shoot and sale?
  • Where does the catalogue work eat the most time?
  • Getting listings onto eBay and the retailers, how much is by hand?

The channels

  • What does the email side look like day to day? What, and how often?
  • Who runs social, and how do you decide what goes out?
  • What is bringing in the most sales right now?

The customer

  • Who is your best customer, and what do they buy first?
  • What do people ask before they buy? (listen for fitment)
♥
The best intel comes off the clock. Lunch, the kettle, the walk to the car. Ease off the questions and just listen.
06

Capture as you go

Tick these off through the day. Your phone remembers them.

  • Names and roles. Who actually does what. Sketch the org.
  • Tools. Shopify apps, email platform, editing software, feed or catalogue tools.
  • Real numbers. List size, monthly orders, conversion, products shot per day. Catch them, do not push.
  • Pain points in their words. Write the quotes down.
  • Photos of the workflow, with permission.
  • The quick-win idea that forms in your head. Note it, do not blurt it.
07

Signals to listen for

  • "It takes ages", "it is a nightmare", "we do that by hand" is a quick win waiting.
  • Any task that is manual, repetitive and high-volume is an automation candidate. At 25,000 SKUs there will be several.
  • Who lights up about growth, and who guards the status quo. That is your champion and your blocker.
!
If you record, get a yes. "I have a little recorder so I do not miss anything, all right if I use it?" On a shoot people are used to being recorded. Never covert.
08

End of day, ten minutes

Before you leave the car park, jot down:

  • The three most useful things you learned.
  • The one bottleneck you would lead with.
  • The quick win you would propose in the sprint.
  • Who decides, and who is your champion.
  • The next step you agreed, and when.
→
Send a short thank-you the same evening. Warm, specific to something from the day, no hard sell. Then hand the recording or notes to Manual Focus to turn into the profile and the opportunity map.
